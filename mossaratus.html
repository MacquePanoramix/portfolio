Featured Project

Mossaratus

Prepare yourself to fight several hordes of enemies. Fortunately, as a robot you’ve got a few extra gun slots to level the odds.

Team size: 4 students Role: I implemented the core shop features (later refined by Ernests) and modelled and fully implemented the boss and the final fight. Scope: This project was completed in about 5 days as the final project for the course “Interactive Media Development”. Tools: Unity and Blender

Overview

Mossaratus is a horde movement shooter where players alternate between fighting waves of monsters and visiting the shop to buy new guns. It was created as part of the course Interactive Media Development, together with three very good teammates.

In Mossaratus, you play as a robot exploring a mossy cave when you are suddenly ambushed by monsters. Your job becomes clearing them out so you can continue your exploration. The game is presented in a CRT-inspired aesthetic. You alternate between wiping out each wave of enemies and using the money they drop to buy items at the shop. We were able to implement all the core features we envisioned for the game, although with more time it would always have been possible to add more content.

Quick facts

  • Genre: horde shooter
  • Platform: PC
  • Engine: Unity
  • Playtime: ~5–10 minutes
  • Team: Luís António de Albuquerque Hollanda, Ernests Malnacs , Edina Depner, Tycho Heijmans

What I’m proud of

Core experience

I feel quite proud of how our game was received during the testing days. Not everyone wanted to play for very long, which is fine, because it wasn’t really made for everyone. However, for the people who did enjoy this style of game, we noticed they got really into it. Some players replayed the game countless times, which is exactly what we were hoping for.

My contribution

I was responsible for setting up the backbone of the shop system and for fully modelling and implementing the boss. Due to a short testing period, the boss fight ended up being harder than we had planned, but I was still very satisfied with the aesthetics of the boss and how it fought. Even though the difficulty is something we would have adjusted if we had more time, it still created interesting experiences for the players who decided to take on the challenge and try to beat it.

What I learned

In this case, I actually learned more from what went right than from what went wrong, because the project went about as well as I could have hoped. I gained a lot of comfort and confidence using Unity and Blender, especially when working with animations.
